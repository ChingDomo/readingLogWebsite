Reflection

The reading reflection of Still Alice.

Still Alice marked a significant milestone for me as it was the first English novel I successfully completed, and its timing felt serendipitous. On one hand, I had developed a fear of English classes during the previous semester, leading to a deep dislike for the language. However, this novel served as a comforting medium that gradually helped me integrate English into my life and foster a genuine appreciation for it. Moreover, through Alice's experiences, I found myself empathizing with situations that were previously foreign to me. This indirect influence permeated my real life, shaping my personal perspective on life and death and impacting my interactions with my family. Reading Still Alice provided me with a sense of relief.

Unlike many other English novels, Still Alice avoids complex language and instead presents a relatable narrative free from excessive technical vocabulary. This played a role in enabling me to finish the book. Additionally, my expanding vocabulary reached a level where I no longer needed to constantly consult a dictionary with every page, allowing me to immerse myself fully in the story.

Thus, delving into Still Alice didn't posed a challenge to reading for me, but my mindset. While empathizing with the characters, particularly Alice, my thoughts often veered towards my own life. Alice is a character I find it difficult to wholeheartedly like, yet her traits resonate with a blend of myself and my mother, creating an intricate emotional connection.

Speaking of my mother, the argument between Alice and Lydia regarding college choices struck a chord within me, vividly reminding me of my final year of high school. During that time, daily debates about selecting a major became the norm. In those three months, I slept for less than two hours per day, allocating the rest of the night to emotional struggles. Ultimately, both my mother and I sought assistance from a psychological clinic. At the age of 17, I considered my mother an unreasonable tyrant and even forcefully altered my preferences on college applications. However, while reading Still Alice, I gained access to Alice's thoughts through the omniscient perspective. As I contemplated her innermost musings, it felt as if I was glimpsing into my own mother's mind. I came to understand that her decisions stemmed from anxiety, driven by the desire for me to embark on the right path without any regrets, as the doors of life were just opening. This realization had a profound impact on me and aided in my journey of letting go.

On the other hand, I see reflections of myself in Alice, particularly in her sense of pride. I have long been aware of my elevated self-esteem, taking pride in my achievements, upbringing in the capital, and various abilities. However, the stark contrast between Alice's Alzheimer's disease and her impressive memory engenders a profound irony within me, which also invokes a sense of helplessness, reinforcing the belief that those abilities were bestowed upon me by fate, and if they can be given, they can be taken away. I deeply empathize with Alice's struggle to maintain her quality, and it is always distressing to contemplate that one day, I too will age, fall ill, or encounter unforeseen accidents, causing these sources of pride to slip away while I remain utterly powerless.

Besides Alice's qualities, the impact of "Still Alice" on my mindset was amplified by its timing in my life when my grandmother, father, and aunt were all battling illnesses. While reading the book, my thoughts were incessantly influenced by the harsh realities of my own experiences. However, paradoxically, it also provided me with solace.

Influenced by my family's struggles, I couldn't avoid confronting the fragility of life and the inevitability of death. The notion of Alice's Butterfly Plan resonated deeply with me—I embraced the idea that life may be fleeting, but it should always be embraced with elegance. Nonetheless, whether it was Alice, my other family members, or my grandmother who also suffered from Alzheimer's, none of them could ultimately navigate their circumstances with grace. This profound sense of helplessness I encountered throughout the book was suffocating, yet it crystallized my understanding that this is the inescapable reality I confront each day. This realization paradoxically empowered me to confront and accept reality, through my connection with the book's world, I recognized that life is inherently intertwined with helplessness and no longer feared it as acutely.

Hence, I would assert that "Still Alice" not only challenged me but also provided a respite—a relief fromthe burdens I carried. It is not a novel that helps people to escape from the harshness of reality.On the contrary, it embodies a striking realism. The act of reading compelled me to reflect upon my own family's experiences, which also facilitated a profound empathic connection with my mother and the universal sense of human helplessness. While I experienced Alice's struggles and helplessness, I gained insight into the understanding that there are others in the world who can genuinely empathize with my own journey. This realization brought me immense relief.

In conclusion, my initial motivation for reading "Still Alice" stemmed from my desire to select an English class that piqued my interest. However, as I immersed myself in the narrative, I discovered that language merely served as a conduit, while the true essence lay in empathy. Although the specific details of "Still Alice" may fade from my memory by this time next year, the enduring recollection will be the profound experience of empathy it evoked within me. At some juncture, the narrative of Alice's life intertwined with my own, and in experiencing her helplessness, I simultaneously processed and alleviated my own pain.
