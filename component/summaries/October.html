October 2003

Summary and two discussions.

As

Alice engaged in a discussion about Dan's work, her remarkable memory shone through as she effortlessly recalled the exact reference, outshining even her fellow professors. However, there was a puzzling moment when she couldn't remember the significance of "Eric" on her to-do list, prompting her to take action and contact her doctor for further evaluation.

October arrived, and it marked Alice's birthday. Her family, John, Anna and Tom, gathered for a celebratory meal, albeit with Lydia's absence. While taking a moment for herself in the restroom, Alice noticed blood in the toilet, indicating that her symptoms were not simply attributed to menopause. This discovery heightened her apprehension and spurred her determination to seek medical attention.

Alice attributed her increasing forgetfulness and disorientation to menopause. However, in the scene in the women's restroom, she discovered that she was still menstruating (p.41). This indicated that there were other underlying issues in her body that were causing her various symptoms. This realization shattered her initial relief, and she found herself confronted with an unknown illness, leading her to break down in tears. At this point, I can empathize with her, as no one can gracefully and calmly face the reality of getting disease.

What can you tell about the relationship between Anna and Lydia? Do you think Alice is aware of the sibling rivalry between her two daughters?

Anna and Lydia have a competitive relationship regarding who is the smartest and most successful daughter in the Howland family (p.38). Anna has always been deeply concerned about this and fears that she may not measure up to Lydia. The reality consistently shows that Anna needs to put in effort to achieve the results that Lydia effortlessly attains. Therefore, Anna feels a sense of happiness when Lydia decides not to attend college because it doesn't threaten her position.

I believe Alice is aware of this situation. My reasoning is that when responding to Anna's question, Alice casually describes Lydia's situation by saying, "She is good." This indicates that Alice doesn't fully agree with Lydia's abilities but doesn't want to fuel Anna's comparisons or diminish her siblings, so she chooses to provide a subtle description.
