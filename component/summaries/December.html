December 2003

Summary and two discussions.

Despite

having a strong bond with her colleagues, whom she considered as family, Alice encountered a disconcerting moment at Eric Wellman's holiday party. She struggled to recall the identity of Dan's wife, raising concerns about her own memory. Determined to understand what was happening, Alice sought the expertise of Dr. Davis, who suspected an underlying issue with her memory. Despite the concerns raised, Alice found it difficult to accept that her memory was deteriorating. She clung to the hope that reviewing photo albums and meticulously recalling details would reassure her of her mental acuity.

However, as the month drew to a close, Alice's memory lapses became more pronounced. In a heartbreaking turn, she even forgot how to make Christmas pudding, a cherished tradition. It was during this time that Lydia, her daughter, may have begun to notice that something was amiss with her mother's cognitive abilities.

For Alice her colleagues were more than colleagues. What did they do together and share? Why do you think that the author arranges this scenario into this month?

Alice and her colleagues have a relationship akin to that of a family. They participate in each other's major life events, such as weddings, births, and the achievements of their children and grandchildren. Additionally, they support one another in their scientific research endeavors. They travel together to various parts of the world to attend conferences. They also provide mutual support in overcoming waves of crippling self-doubt, dealing with illness, and going through divorces.(p.57)

However, due to Alzheimer's disease, I believe Alice will soon lose this "family" that she takes pride in and feels deeply involved with. At this point, she is unaware that her relationship with her colleagues is built on the premise of equality, as they engage in similar work and rely on each other. However, once someone is unable to maintain the same level of quality or ability, I believe this connection will be lost. I think the author has arranged this scene as a foreshadowing of Alice eventually leaving Harvard and her "family" at Harvard.

What went wrong when Alice was preparing the ingredients for the bread pudding? What did Alice do to the eggs? What have you learned or noticed by reading “the massacre” of eggs?

When Alice was preparing bread pudding, she realized that she had forgotten how to make the recipe she had known since childhood. She couldn't remember how many eggs to add, and even with her efforts to recall, she had absolutely no recollection. This incident made the repetitive recitation of various words to test her memory seem meaningless, as it directly proved Alice's memory issues.

Here, I believe this portrays the clinical manifestation of Alzheimer's disease in patients. In the early stages of Alzheimer's, patients may still be able to remember deliberately learned things. This is because these activities involve shorter-term memory processes, which are associated with brain areas less affected, such as the hippocampus. Additionally, these learning activities may also involve more conscious and purposeful attention, allowing patients to better retain these memories in the short term.

However, long-term memories, such as the recipe she had known since childhood, are greatly impacted. Long-term memories primarily involve the hippocampus and temporal lobe, which are often severely degenerated and damaged in Alzheimer's disease. As a result, patients may struggle to recall early personal experiences, childhood events, or specific details from long-term memory.
