September 2004

As

Alice read her students' evaluations alongside Eric, she finally confronted the harsh reality that her Alzheimer's disease had long been impacting her daily performance. It was a sobering realization, and she had to come to terms with the fact that she could no longer continue her beloved role as a teacher. With a mix of sadness and acceptance, she composed a letter to inform all her classmates and colleagues, gracefully bidding farewell to Harvard.

However, accepting her altered circumstances and the loss of her valued position proved to be an arduous process for Alice. She struggled to come to grips with the sense of worth that had been stripped away. Despite this, she chose to stay involved in the academic realm by attending conferences and offering guidance to her students.

During one such conference, though, Alice noticed a marked shift in the dynamics of her relationships. Those whom she had once considered as family in the academic community seemed to shy away, avoiding her company. It became apparent that her condition made her colleagues uncomfortable, leading to a sense of isolation.

Furthermore, at the conference, Alice's cognitive decline manifested in her repetitive speech patterns. While she managed to identify pertinent issues and ask insightful questions, she unwittingly found herself stuck in a loop, repeating the same words over and over again. This repetition not only frustrated Alice but also caused embarrassment among those in attendance.

This experience served as a poignant reminder of the profound impact Alzheimer's had on Alice's once vibrant and engaging presence. It was a painful realization that even in the professional spaces she had thrived in, her disease had become a prominent and inhibiting force, resulting in strained relationships and hindering her ability to participate fully.
