May 2004

Realizing

that her condition would impose other hardships on her family, except the genetic risk, Alice explored the option of a nursing center, examining the facilities and services available there. However, she discovered that specialized Alzheimer's care facilities were prohibitively expensive and patients there had no conversation and no socializing, the only voice was someone singing the same old song all the time. The cost and the state of paitents made her feel terriable, she could not image that one day she would become like that way.

Feeling discouraged, Alice was determined to devise a new plan. Thinking about her mother's words about butterflies having a short but beautiful life, which empathize herself, she decided to do something she had never tried before her diagnosis, like enjoying an ice cream cone. Additionally, she crafted a plan she named "Butterfly," which involved contemplating suicide. She stored "Butterfly" on her computer and set daily reminders on her BlackBerry, which prompted her to answer personal verification questions. Alice believed that implementing this plan would alleviate John's financial burden of caring for a woman who could no longer recognize him, and whom he could no longer recognize in return.

Despite the severity of Alice's cognitive decline, she seemed unaware of the extent to which her condition had deteriorated. She started adding tasks to her to-do list that were more appropriate for her student years. Worser, In one of Alice's classes, instead of taking her usual place at the podium, she opted a seat for students. Failing to recognize that she was the professor and not a student in that moment, Alice made the decision to leave after only twenty minutes, which was due to a formal rule at Harvard.

This alarming progression of Alice's behavior and thought patterns highlighted the urgency and severity of her condition. Her distorted perception of reality and her increasing detachment from her former self were clear signs of the devastating impact of Alzheimer's disease on her life.
